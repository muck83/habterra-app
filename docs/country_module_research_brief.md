# Habterra — Country Module Research Brief

**For:** Research agents (Codex, Cowork, or a human collaborator) commissioned to produce source material for a new country module.
**Output:** A research pack that a content author can turn into a live module using the structure in `india_seed.sql`, `ksa_seed.sql`, `china_seed.sql`, `korea_seed.sql`, `vietnam_seed.sql`, `japan_seed.sql`, `indonesia_seed.sql`, and `uae_seed.sql` as precedent.
**Tone target:** Everything you produce will eventually be read by a parent from the country in question, sitting next to the teacher who is about to teach their child. Write research notes that neither parent nor teacher would find reductive.

---

## 1. What we are building and who it is for

Habterra builds cultural-context modules for international schools. The audience splits cleanly in two:

- **Teachers** at international schools who are about to meet, teach, or assess a student from a country they did not grow up in. Most of these teachers are experienced educators who are not asking for a stereotype sheet — they are asking for the interpretive frame that makes a specific meeting or classroom moment land.
- **Parents** whose children attend those schools. Most of these parents chose an international school as a serious investment in their child's future, and are navigating an assessment system, a vocabulary, and a set of teacher norms that are unfamiliar. They are not asking to be reassured; they are asking for a clear, specific guide to what is actually happening.

Each country module ships in English plus the primary language of the family audience. The teacher and parent versions cover the same underlying material but from each side of the conversation. If your research cannot be written up in a way that serves both audiences honestly, it is not ready.

## 2. What you are researching toward — the module shape

Every country module is built around six dimensions. Your research pack must produce enough sourced material for a content author to fill each dimension with roughly 600–900 words of specific, cited, non-stereotyping analysis.

### D1 — Learning Philosophy
What model of "what learning is" do students arrive with? What is the dominant domestic assessment architecture (structured recall, rote mastery, high-stakes terminal examination, oral recitation, something else) and how does it shape study habits, note-taking, attention to format, willingness to take intellectual risk, and the meaning of a "good student"?

Research questions to answer:
- What is the dominant domestic assessment system, and what does it reward?
- Who designed that system, historically, and what social function was it intended to serve? (Colonial-era, post-independence reform, Cold War modernization, etc.)
- What does a high-performing student in the domestic system look like, behaviorally?
- Which of those behaviors will a teacher in an international school initially misread, and as what?
- What does "95% is not enough" look like in this culture, if it applies?
- Peer-reviewed sources preferred. At minimum one scholarly citation per claim.

### D2 — Parent Communication
How do parents in this culture expect to interact with teachers? What is the expected cadence, medium, register, and topic of parent-teacher contact? What does a "good meeting" look like from their side?

Research questions to answer:
- Is parent involvement expected to be close-monitoring (daily marks, frequent contact) or deferential-at-distance (trust the teacher, do not interfere)?
- What communication channels carry the most weight? (School portal, WhatsApp, parent WeChat, LINE groups, KakaoTalk, Telegram, Viber, SMS, in-person.)
- What is the expected sequence of a parent-teacher meeting? What must be said first for the parent to feel the meeting has actually happened?
- What information do parents in this culture use to evaluate a teacher's seriousness and competence?
- Are there parent information networks (community chat groups, extended family consultation, coaching-center gossip) that shape how your communication will be interpreted *before it reaches the individual parent*?
- What does asking-about-marks mean in this culture — and what is actually being asked?

### D3 — Institutional Context
What does the domestic education landscape *outside the international school* look like, and how does it shape what students bring into class every day?

Research questions to answer:
- What is the domestic curriculum (CBSE, Gaokao, Suneung, Tawjihi, Bagrut, Abitur, Brevet+Bac, SPM, UTBK, ENEM, etc.)?
- What is the shadow education system? (Private tutoring, coaching institutes, hagwons, juku, cram schools, online coaching platforms.) How institutionalized is it? What share of students participate? How many hours per week?
- What are the domestic university entrance examinations and what share of a child's schooling is organized around them?
- What recognition does IB / AP / IGCSE / A-Level have for domestic university admission?
- What are the parallel private-university options (Ashoka-type, Minerva-type, new domestic liberal-arts institutions) that international-school families often consider as safety or first-choice destinations?

### D4 — Ethical Leadership
Where do international teaching ethics and domestic schooling norms meaningfully diverge? Not everything the teacher will encounter is "a different culture"; some of it is a historically specific practice that international ethics actively pushes back on (public ranking, corporal discipline, favoritism based on class or caste, gendered classroom expectations). Research this honestly and non-judgmentally — the teacher needs to understand both why the practice persists and what the right international-school response looks like.

Research questions to answer:
- What classroom practices in the domestic system would feel ethically incompatible with international teaching norms? (Public ranking, class monitors with disciplinary power, favoring "toppers," visible stratification, corporal discipline, differential treatment by gender.)
- Which of these survive as residual expectation or student behavior even when the international school does not practice them?
- What does equity work look like in this cultural context, specifically? (Generic "inclusive classroom" frameworks often fail to name the specific hierarchies that need intervention.)
- Are there class, caste, religion, region, or ethnicity dynamics that shape classroom interaction, peer consultation, who's taken seriously in group work?
- How should a teacher handle a parent request that conflicts with international school policy without being dismissive of the cultural frame behind the request?

### D5 — Student Behavior
What behaviors will teachers see in students from this culture that look like one thing but often are another? This is the interpretive-move dimension — the research that changes how a teacher reads the classroom.

Research questions to answer:
- What does a high-achieving student from this system look like in their first weeks of inquiry-based work?
- What does academic anxiety look like in this culture, and how is it different from Western classroom expressions of academic anxiety?
- How do students in this culture seek help when they are struggling? (Ask publicly, ask privately, ask peers first, ask family, ask coaching tutor, avoid asking entirely.)
- What does "quiet student" mean, specifically? (Confused, socially risk-managing, deferential, disengaged, overloaded.)
- What does "copying" or "plagiarism" mean in this culture's learning tradition, and how should a teacher respond without shaming a behavior the student has been trained to think of as rigorous?
- Are there sleep, coaching-load, or commute patterns that shape attention in class?

### D6 — Difficult Conversations
Five or six recurring parent-teacher or teacher-student flash points that teachers from this region report repeatedly, each with the *common misread*, the *actual dynamic*, and the *interpretive move* that changes the outcome. This is the dimension that makes the module concrete and memorable. Think of it as the "highlight reel" of the whole module.

Format precedent: see `india_seed.sql` D6 ("Five Difficult Conversations"). Each flash point is roughly 150 words and follows the same three-beat structure: what the parent/student says, what it sounds like to a new teacher, what it actually is, and what to do.

Research questions to answer:
- What are the 5–6 conversations teachers report having repeatedly with parents from this country?
- For each, what is the specific cultural frame that produces the parent's question or reaction?
- What is the specific response that acknowledges the parent's concern without compromising international school pedagogy?
- What should a teacher not say, and why?

## 3. Research tone and epistemic standards

**Outcome-focused, not trait-focused.** Avoid sentences that start "Indian parents are…" or "Korean students tend to…". Prefer sentences that start "In a school system where…" or "Parents who have made the choice to…". Traits generalize; systems and choices give teachers something to act on without stereotyping the person in front of them.

**Name systems, not people.** CBSE has a percentage culture; individual Indian parents range from hands-off to obsessive. Gaokao shapes a pressure architecture; individual Chinese students are each their own person inside it. Your job is to explain the architecture so the teacher can read an individual more accurately, not to produce a character sketch of a national type.

**Cite real scholarship.** Each substantive claim needs a source. Preferred sources, roughly in order:
1. Peer-reviewed academic journals in comparative education, sociology of education, cross-cultural psychology.
2. Monograph-length academic books from university presses, UNESCO, OECD, World Bank education reports.
3. Government policy documents (especially the education ministry of the country in question, in the local language if relevant).
4. Major international NGO reports (British Council, ACER, EdWorks, Brookings).
5. Reputable journalism only for ground-truthing current practice — never as a primary source for a cultural claim.

Do not use Wikipedia, LinkedIn posts, or "country guide for expats" blog content as sources. Do not rely on a single Hofstede dimension score as a claim — cite it as context alongside richer research.

**Check for recency and reform.** Many domestic education systems have been actively reforming in the past decade. The Korea you studied in 2005 is not the Korea of 2025. Flag research that may be dated and look for post-2015 updates. Indonesian UTBK replaced SBMPTN in 2023. China's double reduction policy (2021) changed the legal status of shadow education. India's NEP 2020 has not yet fully landed in CBSE schools but has changed the policy frame. Every country has a story like this; your research must reflect the current version, not the textbook version.

**Avoid the "exotic" frame.** If your research reads like it is explaining a foreign culture to a Western audience, you have written the wrong thing. The target reader is a working teacher who is going to meet this family tomorrow, and the parent who is going to sit across from her. Neither of them benefits from exoticization.

**Honor heterogeneity.** Every country is several countries. Urban Jakarta and rural East Java are not the same. Mumbai families and Kerala families are not the same. Kuwaiti nationals and Indian expats living in Kuwait are not the same. Where research on a subgroup is strong, say so; where it generalizes, flag the generalization.

**No lyric-style ethnography.** Habterra is not National Geographic. Avoid poetic descriptions of cultural traits; prefer concrete, dated, referenced claims.

## 4. What the research pack should deliver

For each commissioned country, return a research pack with the following components. This is what the content author needs before they can write the module.

**A. Country brief (1 page).** Current education system overview. Key domestic examinations. Primary curriculum. Language of domestic schooling. Literacy and international-school enrollment numbers. Any major recent reform.

**B. Six dimension memos (500–800 words each).** One per dimension above. Each memo must include:
- 3–5 substantive sections matching the section structure in the India seed file.
- 4–6 concrete, sourced claims per section.
- A citations block at the end listing the specific academic sources used.
- An explicit "interpretive move" paragraph per memo that converts the analysis into a teacher-usable frame.

**C. Five or six flash-point scenarios.** Each scenario is:
- A realistic setup (2–3 sentences, written in specific, concrete terms — a real student name, a real grade level, a real assessment).
- The common misread (one sentence — the wrong frame a teacher defaults to).
- The actual dynamic (2–3 sentences explaining what is really happening).
- A response framework (5–7 bullet points — what to do, in order, with specific language a teacher can use).
- Dimension tags (which of D1–D6 the scenario draws from).

**D. Parent-side translation notes.** For each of the six dimensions, a 150–200 word note written directly *to the parent*, explaining the same dynamic from the family's side — translated into the family's language at publish time, drafted in English at research time. This is what makes Habterra different from teacher-only PD: the parent version of the module has to answer the parent's actual questions, not just explain the teacher's position.

**E. Source bibliography.** Full academic citations in a single block — author, year, title, publisher/journal, DOI where available. At least 15 scholarly sources per country, drawn from at least 5 distinct authors. Flag any source that is more than 15 years old and explain why it is still load-bearing.

**F. Red-flag register.** Anything you encountered in research that would turn a parent, teacher, or admin off if we wrote it into a module. This is explicitly for the content author to avoid. Examples: outdated caste generalizations, stereotypes about religious practice, dated claims about gender dynamics, oversimplified political framings, colonial-era tropes that persist in older scholarship.

## 5. How to source well for a country you have not researched before

Start with the country's national education ministry website. Find the current curriculum framework document. Find the most recent national assessment report. These are primary sources and they anchor everything else.

Next, find the two or three comparative education scholars who have worked on this country specifically — not regional specialists, but country specialists. Comparative Education Review, the International Journal of Educational Development, and Compare are good journals to mine. Google Scholar searches in the form `"[country] education" site:.edu OR site:.gov` and then refined by year (post-2015) are usually more productive than generic searches.

For diaspora context — which matters because international-school families are often diaspora or transnationally mobile — look for scholarship on migration and education. Minzu University (China), JNU (India), and IISS (Indonesia) have strong scholars working on this in local languages. If you can read the local language, read the local language; translations of the research landscape into English are often thin and dated.

For shadow-education research specifically, Mark Bray's work at the University of Hong Kong is the globally recognized anchor, and his citations will lead you to country-specific shadow-education scholars.

For current practice, look for recent teacher-training documents from the country's leading teacher-education institutions — these often contain the clearest statement of what the system currently expects of teachers, which tells you what parents currently expect of schools.

## 6. Countries we have not yet built and why each matters

Listed here so you can pick up an assignment without additional context.

- **Philippines.** Large OFW parent base, rising international-school enrollment in Manila and Cebu. Education system restructured significantly under K-12 reform (2013 onward). English-medium domestic schooling creates a different translation problem than non-English countries.
- **Thailand.** High international-school density in Bangkok, Chiang Mai, Phuket. Strong domestic university pathway plus substantial study-abroad aspiration. Deference-to-teacher culture that looks compliant but masks specific anxieties.
- **Malaysia.** Multi-ethnic education landscape (Malay, Chinese, Indian), vernacular schooling tracks, SPM examinations, strong international-school uptake particularly in KL and Penang.
- **Singapore.** Extreme academic-streaming culture, recent reforms toward reduced ranking, strong local-school system that international schools compete against. Parents here are among the most educated consumers of international schooling anywhere — the module has to match their level.
- **Nigeria.** Africa's largest economy, rapidly growing international-school market in Lagos and Abuja. WAEC examinations, strong religious-schooling tradition, complex fee-paying secondary landscape, significant diaspora to UK/US universities.
- **Kenya.** East Africa's strongest private-school sector, KCSE examinations, rapidly growing IB uptake, distinctive mission-school heritage that shapes many international schools.
- **Egypt.** Regional leader in private schooling, Thanawiya Amma examinations, rising IB and American Diploma demand. The role of *darsa khsossy* (private tutoring) is institutionally significant.
- **Turkey.** Large educated middle class, distinctive examination culture (YKS, formerly ÖSS), strong private-school sector including foreign-language-medium schools with long history (German, French, Italian, British, American).
- **Brazil.** Portuguese-speaking, largely disconnected from the English-first international-school conversation, ENEM-shaped study culture, growing IB adoption in São Paulo and Rio, distinctive Catholic-private-school tradition.
- **Mexico.** Large UNAM-shaped domestic system, rising interest in IB for study-abroad pathways, significant bi-national families, El Bachillerato shaping secondary assessment.
- **Pakistan.** O-Level/A-Level heritage, strong private-schooling sector, distinctive class dynamics inside elite international schools, Federal Board and Cambridge board co-existence.
- **Bangladesh.** Rapidly growing middle class, English-medium schools with British and American curriculum, distinctive examination (HSC) pressure, Dhaka-centric international-school market.

This list is not exhaustive and is not a priority order. Mark will assign specific countries based on school partnerships.

## 7. Anti-patterns — research we are actively not commissioning

If you find yourself writing any of the following, stop and reframe:

- A list of Hofstede dimension scores with commentary. (Context, not content.)
- A "Do's and Don'ts" list of cultural etiquette. (Reductive; the wrong register for this audience.)
- An explainer that contrasts "the [country] approach" with "the Western approach." (False binary; most international schools are not Western and most domestic systems are not monolithic.)
- Anything that reduces parent behavior to a stereotype about tiger parents, pushy parents, absent parents, or "helicopter" parents. (Names, not analysis.)
- A summary of a country's history of colonization used as the explanatory frame for everything that happens in a modern classroom. (Colonial legacy is real and relevant; it is not the only lens.)
- Content that an admissions officer would remove from the website on legal advice.

Deliver each country as a single folder under `docs/research/<country-slug>/` containing:

- `01_country_brief.md`

- `03_scenarios.md`
- `04_parent_translation_notes.md`
- `05_bibliography.md`
- `06_red_flag_register.md`
- `notes_raw.md` — optional scratch file for the author if useful

Cite every claim inline with author-year shortform; keep the full citation in the bibliography. When you are uncertain about a claim, flag it with `[UNCERTAIN — verify with country specialist]` rather than soften the language. It is much easier for the content author to chase a flagged uncertainty than to reverse a confident-sounding error.

## 9. A worked example of the voice to aim for

Not this:

> *Chinese parents value education very highly. They expect their children to study hard and often push them to succeed. The Gaokao is a very important exam in China.*

This:

> *The Gaokao is the single national examination that determines university admission for roughly 11 million Chinese students each year, with tiered results tied directly to institution rank. For a family who has built a decade of schooling around this architecture, a report card from an international school that does not produce a ranked comparison is not just unfamiliar — it is missing the piece of information the parent had been trained to look for. This is what is actually being asked when a parent from a Gaokao-shaped background asks "how is my child doing compared to the class." The question is not aggression; it is the parent trying to locate their child on the only coordinate system they have.*

The second version is longer, specific, and respectful. It names a system, not a type of person. It acknowledges that the parent's question has internal logic without excusing any specific behavior. It gives the teacher something to do with the information. That is the target.
